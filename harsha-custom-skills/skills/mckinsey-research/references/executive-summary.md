# Executive Summary Construction — McKinsey Bold-Bullet Format

## Purpose

The executive summary is the SINGLE most important output element. McKinsey partners
read ONLY the executive summary for 90% of research reports. If the executive summary
fails, the entire report fails — regardless of the quality of underlying research.

## SCR Framework for Executive Summary

### Situation (25-30% of executive summary)
- Current landscape, context, baseline — what the reader already knows
- Set the stage WITHOUT judgment — neutral, factual
- Include quantitative anchors: market size, growth rate, current adoption
- Name the specific entity/market/technology being examined

**Template:**
> [Entity/Market] currently [baseline metric or state]. Over the past [timeframe],
> [relevant trend or context]. [Key stakeholder] faces [current challenge or question].

### Complication (embedded in first 25-30%)
- What changed? What broke? Why act NOW?
- Create urgency — the cost of inaction
- Introduce tension that demands resolution
- Should be specific, not vague doom ("disruption is coming" = bad)

**Template:**
> However, [specific change/event] has [specific impact]. This creates [specific risk/opportunity]
> that requires [type of decision] within [timeframe]. The cost of inaction is [specific consequence].

### Resolution (60-70% of executive summary)
- THE answer — specific, sequenced, resourceable
- Connected to business metrics the audience tracks
- Not vague recommendations ("consider investing in AI") — specific actions
- Include expected outcomes and conditions

**Template:**
> Our analysis of [scope] across [source count] sources identifies [number] key findings:
> [Bold-bullet findings follow]

## Bold-Bullet Format

The critical innovation: C-suite executives can grasp the complete argument by
scanning **BOLD TEXT ONLY** in 2-3 minutes.

```markdown
## Executive Summary

**[Situation + Complication in 2-3 sentences: what's happening and why it matters NOW]**

**[Bold finding #1 — the most important conclusion as a complete sentence]**
- Supporting evidence with specific data point (Source: [attribution])
- Second supporting point with quantitative backing
- Caveat or condition under which this finding might not hold

**[Bold finding #2 — second most important conclusion]**
- Supporting evidence with specific data
- Source attribution
- Key limitation or uncertainty

**[Bold finding #3 — third most important conclusion]**
- Supporting evidence
- Source attribution
- Relevant caveat

**[Bold recommendation — specific, sequenced action with expected outcome]**
- Action step 1 with timeline and expected metric impact
- Action step 2 with resource requirements
- Fallback plan if primary recommendation fails
```

## Scanning Test

After writing the executive summary, apply this test:

1. Read ONLY the bold text — does it tell a complete, coherent story?
2. Can a C-suite executive make a decision from bold text alone?
3. Does the bold text follow a logical sequence (finding → finding → action)?
4. Would removing any bullet point change the conclusion? If not, remove it.

## SCQA Variant (For Maximum Engagement)

When the audience needs extra narrative pull:

- **Situation**: Context they already know
- **Complication**: The problem or change
- **Question**: "Given this, what should we do?" (creates explicit tension)
- **Answer**: The resolution — now the reader WANTS this information

Use SCQA when the topic is counter-intuitive or the recommendation challenges
existing assumptions. The explicit question creates buy-in before the answer.

## Common Executive Summary Failures

### Failure 1: Situation-Heavy (80% context, 20% answer)
- **Symptom**: Three paragraphs of background before any finding
- **Fix**: Force SCR ratio — 25-30% situation, 60-70% resolution
- **Test**: Is the first finding visible without scrolling?

### Failure 2: Vague Recommendations
- **Symptom**: "Companies should consider investing in..."
- **Fix**: Specific actions with metrics: "Allocate $X to Y within Z months, targeting N% improvement in metric M"
- **Test**: Could someone execute this recommendation TODAY?

### Failure 3: Missing the "So What?"
- **Symptom**: Findings without implications — "Market grew 15%"
- **Fix**: Every finding needs a "this means" component
- **Test**: After each finding, ask "so what?" — if unanswerable, add implication

### Failure 4: Kitchen Sink (everything included)
- **Symptom**: 10+ findings crammed into the summary
- **Fix**: Rule of Three — exactly 3-5 key findings, MECE organized
- **Test**: Could you explain this to a CEO in an elevator ride?

### Failure 5: Non-MECE Findings
- **Symptom**: Findings that overlap or leave gaps
- **Fix**: MECE test — do findings cover 100% of the topic with zero overlap?
- **Test**: "If I add all findings together, is the full picture covered?"

## Confidence Display in Executive Summary

Always include confidence indicators, but DON'T let them dominate:

**In-line approach (preferred for executive summary):**
> **Cloud migration reduces infrastructure costs by 30-45% for mid-market companies**
> - Based on analysis of 12 case studies and 3 industry surveys (High confidence)
> - Effect size varies significantly by industry vertical (±15%)

**Footnote approach (for cleaner scanning):**
> **Cloud migration reduces infrastructure costs by 30-45%** ¹
> ¹ High confidence — 12 case studies, 3 industry surveys, ±15% variance by vertical

## Length Guidelines

| Report Length | Executive Summary Target |
|---|---|
| 5-10 pages | 1/2 page (150-200 words) |
| 10-25 pages | 1 page (300-400 words) |
| 25-50 pages | 1-2 pages (400-700 words) |
| 50+ pages | 2 pages maximum (700 words cap) |

The executive summary should NEVER exceed 2 pages regardless of report length.
If it does, you're including too much detail — push it to the findings section.

## Assembly Process

1. **Write findings section FIRST** — never write the executive summary first
2. **Extract the 3-5 most important findings** — MECE organized
3. **Write the recommendation** — what should the reader DO?
4. **Write the complication** — why does this matter NOW?
5. **Write the situation** — minimal context needed to understand
6. **Apply bold-bullet format** — bold = claim, bullet = evidence
7. **Run scanning test** — bold text alone tells complete story
8. **Check SCR ratio** — 25-30% situation, 60-70% resolution
9. **Trim ruthlessly** — every sentence must earn its place
